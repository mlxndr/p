Where Are We?

  • Last week, we looked at how clauses contain phrases, and how those phrases have a function within a clause (Subject, Predicator, Object, Complement, Adverbial)
  • Recap:
    • Phrase structure is about the internal structure of phrases (eg inside a NP, what functions as modifier, headword or neither?)
    • Clause structure is about how phrases combine in larger structures, such as sentences (eg is the NP functioning as the Subject or the Object of a clause?)

The Levels of Grammar

(The 'Rank Scale')

  • Sentence: Made up of main clause(s) plus possible subordinate clause(s)
  • Clause: Made up of phrases acting as Subject/Predicator/Object/Complement/Adverbial
  • Phrase: Made up of words acting as headwords or modifiers in a Noun Phrase/Verb Phrase/Prepositional Phrase/Adverb Phrase/etc.
  • Word: Noun/Verb/Adjective/Preposition, etc.

Okay, what is a clause?

  • The key characteristic of a clause is that it must contain a VP functioning as Predicator
  • We can think of clauses as being built up around Predicators; a clause is essentially a Predicator with its associated (optional) SOCA elements
  • Often, but by no means always, the basic structure is SPOC with As cropping up anywhere in between
  • And the main thing about clauses is that they are the elements which make up a sentence

Simple and complex grammatical styles

Different grammatical styles (simple and complex):

  • have different processing demands
  • have a different degree of formality
  • are associated with speech (simple) and writing (complex)
  • signal clause relationships differently
